propaganda poster

project two

ARTS 102

online

You will be creating a propaganda poster advocating for a cause you choose that explores the design principles of Rhythm, Line, and Shape.

You will create a Propaganda Poster on an 11” x 17” document, horizontal or vertical, your choice of orientation. You will research the idea of Propaganda Posters for ideas and thematic choices based on your personal beliefs and preferences. Take a stand and convince others through your art.

Your topic can be deep and meaningful such as advocating for an End To War or an End To Racism, but it can also be silly and light, such as advocating for Pizza for Breakfast or Taco Tuesday to become a weekly national holiday. Your poster can be positive by calling for something good to happen, such as Free College For All or it can be negative by calling for the banning of something like No More Single-Use Plastic.

research propaganda inspiration

Take the time to familiarize yourself with how propaganda posters have been created. There is a rich history of quality graphic design for both positive and negative propaganda. Most people think of the posters from the world wars, however protest posters from Vietnam can be just as powerful. More recently, look at protest posters from Black Lives Matter or Gay Pride. Politics, of course, have a strong history of posters and symbolism as well. Just look at the most recent Biden/Trump election cycle for examples.

Graphically, you can examine the work of by Steve Shepard, Jenny Holzer, Barbara Kruger as inspirational examples. Also, Google Images can be your friend.

topic and storytelling considerations

  • Choose a topic that piques your interest, or evokes a strong emotion for you as an artist.
  • Choose a social or political quote from your topic that evokes your/your reader’s emotion the best. Create a meaningful slogan
  • Make sure your information is reliable.
  • Depict your statement on a poster through the use of imagery and text.
  • Your image should be an “in your face” style; the image should be large and possibly cropped, going off the edge of the paper or depicting an unusual angle or scene… Think about what point of view you want to depict.
  • Add minimal text (See art of Barbara Kruger) – or make it all about the words (see Jenny Holzer).
  • Remember that the more text you use the more time it takes for the viewer to “get it”. Sometimes simple is best.
  • Choose your color scheme carefully. You may want to choose a monochromatic color scheme (all blues, all reds, etc.)
  • Research imagery of elements you wish to include in your poster.

agreement to be open-minded

As we work on these posters it is important to remember that we will be sharing a variety of points of view. We do not have to agree with the topic to be able to evaluate the work. In your research, you may find posters with topics you find distasteful, but you can still appreciate them for their graphic design potential. For example, the Nazis were horrible people and we should rightly judge them negatively, however their graphic design and presentation of messaging was quite well done.

Similarly, choose topics that you feel strongly about even if that means potentially offending someone else. These posters do not need to be politically correct or non-offensive. The point of propaganda is to take a side on a topic which will naturally upset others on the opposite side. That being said, be consciously aware of what you are saying and how it is being said. Be sure there is a meaningful reason for your design.

Also be aware that others may be creating posters that you find upsetting. Please keep an open mind about this. We don't need to agree on content, but we do need to agree to keep an open mind and be accepting of alternative viewpoints.

principles & elements

In addition to exploring the social aspects of propaganda, you will also be exploring how to use the design principles in visual design. Your understanding of design concepts will lead to a stronger visual message.

Do not forget the lessons from the previous project. You should still be aware of how you use unity, balance, and emphasis in your designs. Even though you are not necessarily being graded on them specifically, they still affect your design.

rhythm

The principle of Rhythm is about using repetition of elements to create a sense of flow. The repetition can be used to create regular patterns for textures or it could be used to create movement to guide the viewer's eye across the page. The repeated hands scaling up in size creates a solid sense of movement and power in the composition.

You can also use repetition of elements to build a sense of unity through the consistent colors or objects. For example, the repeated hands holding money creates a sense of community with the green color alluding to the color of dollar bills.

line

The principle of Line is about the actual physical lines in the illustration, such as the outlines of the hands holding money in the image. The lines are strong and straight, but have some variation in width which adds character. You can see that lines and shapes often have considerable overlap in presentation. The lines of the bills have a lower contrast and are there to illustrate money, as well as to create repeated elements that form a strong line moving upward. The background buildings are faded out, but still have repeated shapes that build lines moving upward. Everything feels powerful and strong, which is the point of the piece.

shape

The principle of Shape is about building objects that have mass and takes up space. Lines can outline a shape, of course, but a solid mass of color or texture has considerably more visual weight. Shapes can be solid or made up of smaller elements (proximity). Shapes can be repeated to create implied lines or visual rhythms. In the poster, the bold shapes of money dominate the scene. The hands are also emphasized, but it is the money that captures the attention. The repetition of those elements builds a flow that moves upward in a powerful way. The bold font creates strong letter shapes that form a solid base to the composition that communicates the message in a very literal way.

process

pre-planning & research

The first step is to figure out what you want to say and how you would like to possibly go about saying it. You don't need to formally answer these questions however it is worthwhile to consider the following:

  • What is the topic of your project?
  • How is it relevant to you?
  • What type of imagery do you plan to use?
  • Where will you get your imagery?
  • How will you tell your story with imagery & composition?
  • What will you title your composition?

Of course, you do not need to have answers to these questions from the start. Use the questions to guide your preliminary explorations. Spend time researching both propaganda in general and your topic specifically. See what has been done before and let that influence what you choose to do. Collect example images of styles or techniques you like. Find images for things that you will need for your poster, such as a pro-coffee poster would potentially need images of coffee cups, coffee makers, coffee houses, coffee packaging, hipsters and "cool" people. Maybe pictures of cookies, scones, muffins, tea stuff, etc.

design activities

These are the sketches and doodles and other visual explorations where you experiment with your different ideas. Play around with positioning of elements, colors, slogans, etc. Break out your markers and colored pencils to build color schemes. Print out images from your computer and color on top of them. Be sure to specifically study how rhythm, line, and shape will be used to build your visual statement.

You are not limited to purely traditional methods for exploring ideas. You can use Photoshop or other digital art tools to try things out. Keep things in layers and move them around the screen. Make things bigger or smaller. Shift colors. Try out fonts. Play with filters.

A blend of type and image is preferred. You may use color or stick with black and white imagery (perhaps mostly b&w with a good splash of color for emphasis?). Keep readability in mind. Fonts choices matter to sell the idea. Check out free fonts for commercial use for new fonts you probably don't have installed on your computer. I really like font squirrel as a starting place.

Building off Project 1, think of this poster as a controlled collage of elements for a specific purpose. Keeping that collage mentality during your process can help you find a good composition and visual statement.

final poster

Your final digital file will be a Photoshop file that is 11" x 17" RGB color mode set to 72ppi.

Beyond that, there are no real limits here. Make something meaningful with the tools and skills you have at your disposal. You may not achieve museum-quality work and that is fine. Grading is primarily on utilization of design principles and making a creative statement.
