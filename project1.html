project one

ARTS 102

online

You will be creating two digital collages exploring the design principles of Unity, Emphasis, and Balance.

Content, style, tools, and artistic statement are the artist's choice. You get the freedom to make what you want and say what you want to say.

principles & elements

unity

The principle of Unity is about finding common themes, whether visual or conceptual. Each element of the collage will help contribute to building the overall whole.

Collage 1 should explore visual unity through objects that have similar visual characteristics. It doesn't matter what the objects actually are, they just need to look the same. For example, things that are red could be stop signs, Coke cans, buckets of blood, Santa hats, taillights, candy canes, etc. These are all red, but are very different objects. You still want contrast, so don't make every single element red. Use other colors as a complement, such as a green field with red flowers (made up of candy canes and Santa hats, of course).

Collage 2 should explore conceptual unity through objects that have similar ideas, things that are the same no matter what they look like. For example, a collection of vehicles could be things like cars, boats, airplanes, skateboards, pogo-sticks, rocketships, etc. These all look very different, but are all things that can take you to new places.

emphasis

While Unity is about bringing things together, the principle of Emphasis is about making individual elements stand out. The essential trick is to establish a visual pattern and then break that pattern. For example, a bunch of small, round, and flowing objects contrasted by one big, square, jagged element.

Both collages should have one element or grouping that acts as a clear focal point of the composition. Other elements in the composition should act as accents that point the viewer's eye toward that focal point. Note that Radial symmetry can create immediate focal points because there's an obvious central point to the pattern.

balance

The principle of Balance is about making sure all the elements in the composition feel arranged and positioned in just the right way. Sometimes a composition wants to feel energetic, as if the elements are dynamically moving. Other times, a composition wants to feel relaxed and static, as if the elements are completely at rest and happy right where they are. We can use Symmetry and Asymmetry as tricks to balance the compositions.

You should have one collage that is Symmetric (clear line of reflection) and one collage that is Asymmetric (no clear line of reflection). A symmetric reflection does not need to be placed exactly horizontally or vertically and does not need to be in the center of the page. Similarly, an asymmetric design could include smaller elements that are symmetric. It is all about placement of elements in the overall composition.

artistic statement

Choosing elements and arranging them according to the design principles is only part of the project. You also need to consider what are you saying and how are you saying it. Randomly scattering your elements across the page just to fill the space is the boring first response many new designers have. Please don't do this.

Instead, take the time to think about how you can arrange those elements into patterns or bigger groupings. Can you create recognizable shapes out of those groupings? For example, those vehicles listed above can be grouped into giant robots having a battle. The cars and boats and pogo-sticks become arms and legs of the robots. Or all those red elements could become birds lined up on a tree branch.

Finally, consider what visual story you are telling with your designs. Is the robot battle a fair fight? Is there one big robot fighting a bunch of smaller robots or is it two equally matched bots? Is one side clearly winning or are they just about to fight in one of those epic stand-off scenes you always see on movie posters? Is there are good side we should be a rooting for?

Of course, your stories do not need to be that complex or detailed. Maybe your red birds are just happy to be sitting on that tree branch. If so, how can you let us know it is a comfortable and wonderful day to be a bird? Or maybe it is a dark day because a fellow bird-friend is missing? How would you show that?

You are not obligated to make recognizable objects or tell specific stories with characters. Maybe you want to create a line of elements flowing around the page. Does the line feel like it moves quickly or slowly? Are there gaps in it? Does it curve a lot or is it move in straight lines? Are there two lines interacting, intersecting, breaking into each other? What is the background like and how does it contrast with the elements? Does it feel happy or sad or scary?

Basically, the point here is to do something intentional and meaningful with your collages. Be aware of how your elements interact with each other. Where are there harmonies and where are there tensions? How can you control that to make an interesting image that is more than just random elements on the page?

process

The desire is to have a beautiful final image, however, the point of this project is to follow the creative process and explore the design principles. Working through the steps makes it much more likely a quality finished image will result. Many newbies will jump blindly into an idea and rush straight to making something without really considering the possibilities. This can sometimes work, but rarely can it be relied on to be successful.

The most important part of the process is to get your ideas out of your head and into a tangible form. Write things down, sketch them out, make doodles, scribble, make lists, etc. The format doesnâ€™t matter, just do whatever makes sense to you. The focus is on the ideas, not the quality of the marks. A bunch of scribbles and arrows can be critically important if they are meaningful to you.

practical stuff

Here are the basics:

  • Size: a rectangular image not larger than 8"x10" so that it could be printed on a letter-sheet of paper. Adjust proportions as appropriate for the art. A tall, skinny design can be just as good as a square composition.
  • Resolution: 300 ppi/dpi.
  • Digital Software: instructions will be given for using Photoshop, however it is not required. Any digital art software you choose to use should be fine.
  • Files: master layered files and flattened image files.
